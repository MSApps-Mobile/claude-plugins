# Account-based marketing (ABM)

ABM is the go-to-market motion where **sales and marketing pick a defined set of best-fit, highest-value accounts and run personalized plays to win them.** It's not "marketing to companies" — it's a coordinated, account-by-account campaign with explicit named-target lists and shared revenue goals.

For a Shopify Partner, ABM is how you go after the **20 dream merchants** you'd love to migrate to Plus this year — instead of broadcast marketing to 200,000 Shopify stores.

## ABM is account-first, not channel-first

The defining shift: in traditional demand-gen you start with a channel ("what's our LinkedIn budget?"). In ABM you start with a **target list** and design plays around each account.

Steps:
1. Pick the accounts (named list).
2. Tier them (1:1 / 1:few / 1:many).
3. Map the buying committee at each account.
4. Build a per-account or per-cluster plan.
5. Coordinate sales + marketing on the same accounts.
6. Measure pipeline by account.

## The three ABM tiers — pick by deal size and team capacity

| Tier | Target volume | Personalization | Best for | Cost / account |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **1:1 strategic** | 5–25 accounts | Fully bespoke (custom microsites, exec gifts, named landing pages) | $250k+ deals, multi-year contracts, named-brand merchants | High |
| **1:few** | 25–100 accounts | Cluster-level (industry kits, role-specific assets, shared use case) | $50k–250k deals, vertical-focused plays | Medium |
| **1:many** | 100–1,000 accounts | Segment-level (programmatic, dynamic web content) | $10k–50k deals, broader reach | Low |

The Academy emphasizes: **don't run 1:1 ABM if you don't have the team, budget, and exec sponsorship for it.** Most small partners should start at 1:few and graduate to 1:1 only on hand-picked dream accounts.

## Picking the target list

The right number of named accounts depends on tier × close rate × cycle. A 1:few list of 50 accounts at 10% close = 5 deals over a 6–9 month cycle. Build the list by combining:

- **ICP-fit accounts** from technographic data (BuiltWith, Wappalyzer, Shopify Plus public listings).
- **Trigger signals** — recent funding, new ecommerce hire, expansion into a new market, platform-migration signals (e.g., DNS pointing at WooCommerce + a new Shopify Plus inquiry).
- **Existing relationship signals** — warm intros, mutual connections, past prospects who said "not now".
- **Strategic value** — case-study potential, referral potential, brand halo.

## Mapping the buying committee

For each target account, identify:

- **Champion** — the person who wants the change (often VP Ecom or Head of Eng).
- **Economic buyer** — signs the contract (often founder or COO).
- **End user** — uses what you ship (merchandiser, marketing ops).
- **Blocker** — could veto (often legal, IT, or CFO).

A 1:1 ABM play touches all 4. A 1:few play touches the champion + economic buyer.

## Plays by tier — concrete examples

### 1:1 play — "Brand X Migration Microsite"
- Custom landing page with the merchant's logo, product imagery, a mocked-up Plus storefront preview.
- An exec briefing book showing their store on Plus with projected revenue uplift.
- Direct outreach from the partner founder to the merchant founder.
- Optional: a physical send (printed audit, branded swag).
- Sales rep + marketer + senior tech meet weekly on the account.

### 1:few play — "DTC Fashion Migration Cohort"
- Industry-themed landing page ("Plus migrations for fashion DTC").
- Case-study kit featuring 2–3 wins in the segment.
- Webinar with 3 fashion-specific guests.
- LinkedIn ads targeting the cluster.
- Personalized email mentioning each merchant's specific store name.
- Outreach cadence by SDR with manager-level escalation when high-intent.

### 1:many play — "Plus Readiness Assessment"
- Programmatic landing page with a self-serve audit tool.
- Display + LinkedIn ads to the broad ICP segment.
- Lifecycle email nurture by stage.
- Inbound MQLs handed to SDRs with light qualification.

## Shared sales + marketing alignment

ABM falls apart without alignment. The Academy stresses:

- **Same target list.** Sales and marketing must agree on the named accounts.
- **Same definitions.** What's an MQL? What's an SQL? Write it down.
- **Same KPIs.** Pipeline by named account, not "leads generated".
- **Same cadence.** Weekly account-status review across both teams.
- **Same content.** Marketing produces what sales actually uses.

Misalignment shows up as: marketing reports "great engagement" while sales reports "no real meetings" — that's a sign the target list is wrong or content isn't sales-ready.

## ABM-specific KPIs

Standard demand-gen metrics (impressions, leads, MQLs) miss the point. Use:

- **Account engagement rate** — % of named accounts with ≥3 touches recorded.
- **Account meetings** — # of named accounts with a meeting booked.
- **Account pipeline** — $ pipeline created from named-account conversations.
- **Deal velocity** — time from first touch to closed-won within named accounts.
- **Account expansion** — $ from named accounts post-close (renewals, upsells).
- **Win rate vs. non-ABM** — close rate on named accounts vs. inbound, to validate the approach.

## ABM and the Shopify Partner Program

Three Shopify-specific ABM levers:

1. **Co-marketing with Shopify.** Tier-2+ partners can co-author content, co-host webinars, and get access to Shopify's BD team for warm intros to enterprise prospects.
2. **App ecosystem partnerships.** ABM with complementary apps (e.g., Klaviyo, Recharge, Gorgias) — joint plays into accounts using both your services and the partner app — multiply the touch surface area.
3. **Partner directory bid.** Optimize listing for vertical + region; named-account research often starts there.

## Common ABM mistakes

- **Starting at 1:1 with too few resources.** Bespoke plays drain time; without 1–2 closes you'll abandon the program.
- **Treating ABM as just a marketing campaign.** It's a sales-and-marketing motion. If sales isn't on the list, it's not ABM.
- **Static target list.** Rotate accounts in/out quarterly — some won't progress, others become hot.
- **Skipping the buying-committee map.** Talking only to the champion = the deal stalls at the economic buyer.
- **Not capturing engagement data.** If you don't track which named accounts touched which content, you can't optimize.

## When ABM is *not* the answer

- Self-serve products (apps, templates) — broad demand-gen wins.
- Very small deals — economics don't justify it.
- Greenfield / no ICP yet — pick ICP first.
- No team to run it — undercommitted ABM produces worse results than well-run broad outreach.

## Output checklist

- [ ] Tier chosen (1:1 / 1:few / 1:many) and matches team capacity.
- [ ] Target list named, sized correctly for the tier.
- [ ] Trigger signals identified for entry/exit on the list.
- [ ] Buying committee mapped per account.
- [ ] Tier-appropriate play designed (microsite / cluster kit / programmatic).
- [ ] Sales and marketing aligned on list, KPIs, and cadence.
- [ ] Account-level KPIs (not lead-level) instrumented.
- [ ] Quarterly rotation rule defined.
